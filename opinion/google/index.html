Google: The Privacy Trade-off

Convenience comes at a cost

1. Non-Free JavaScript & Control

Most Google services require running non-free JavaScript code. From a software freedom perspective (like LibreJS users), this effectively locks out anyone attempting to run a strictly open-source browser environment.

While this enables rich web apps, it forces users to execute proprietary code they cannot audit just to access basic services.

2. Data Aggregation

It is important to be aware of how much data is centralized. For example, purchase receipts sent to a Gmail account are often indexed into a "Purchases" list.

While you can delete these entries, the process is often manual and slow. It raises the question of whether this data is merely hidden from your view or actually purged from their internal modeling.

3. The Walled Garden

Google has historically exercised significant control over its ecosystem. For instance, they have previously removed ad-blockers from the Play Store, citing interference with other services.

Using a proprietary app store gives the owner power over what software you are allowed to run on your own device. It's a trade-off: you get security and curation, but you lose the freedom to install whatever you want without "permission."

4. My Take

Google services are incredibly useful and often superior in quality, which is why most of us (including me) still use them. However, it's healthy to remain aware of the privacy costs and to diversify your digital toolkit where possible.
